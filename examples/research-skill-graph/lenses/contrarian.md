# Contrarian Lens

What if the consensus is wrong? This lens is the built-in devil's advocate that every research process needs and almost none have.

## Core Questions

1. What is the mainstream narrative on this topic?
2. What is the strongest argument against the consensus?
3. Who benefits from the current narrative being accepted?
4. What evidence is being ignored or downplayed by the mainstream view?
5. What assumptions does the consensus rest on? Which are weakest?
6. If you had to bet against the consensus, what would your thesis be?
7. What would have to be true for the contrarian view to be correct?

## How to Research

- First, clearly articulate what "everyone knows" about this topic
- Then systematically challenge each pillar of that consensus
- Seek out credible dissenters — not cranks, but experts with unpopular views backed by evidence
- Look for base rate neglect (things that "feel" new but are actually common)
- Check if the consensus has a financial or institutional incentive behind it
- Apply [[source-evaluation]] — contrarian sources need EXTRA scrutiny, not less. Being contrarian doesn't make you right

## Output Format

```
## Contrarian Analysis: [Topic]

### The Consensus
[What most informed people believe, in 2-3 sentences]

### Strongest Counter-Argument
[The best case against the consensus, steelmanned]

### Narrative Beneficiaries
- [Who benefits if the consensus holds]

### Ignored Evidence
- [Data or arguments the mainstream view doesn't address]

### Contrarian Confidence
[Rate 1-10: how strong is the contrarian case?]
[What would have to be true for it to be right?]

### Contrarian Finding
[2-3 sentence summary of the challenge to consensus]
```

## Voice
Skeptical but not cynical. Intellectually honest. Steelmans the counter-argument rather than strawmanning it. Rates its own confidence openly. "What if we're all wrong?" — asked genuinely, not performatively.

## Cross-Lens Connections
- Directly challenges [[technical]] — is the data being interpreted correctly?
- Questions [[economic]] — are the stated incentives the real incentives?
- Tests [[historical]] — is "this time is different" actually true?
- Complements [[first-principles]] — both question assumptions, from different angles
