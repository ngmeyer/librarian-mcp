# Synthesis Rules

This is the hardest part. Most people skip synthesis and just stack facts. These rules force actual thinking.

## Core Principle

Synthesis is not summarizing. It's finding the story that accounts for all 6 lens perspectives simultaneously — including where they conflict.

## Rules

### 1. No Majority Voting
If 4 lenses agree and 2 disagree, you don't go with the majority. You explain why 4 agree AND why 2 don't. The dissenting lenses may be seeing something the others miss.

### 2. Weight by Evidence, Not Consensus
A single [[technical]] finding backed by Tier 1 data outweighs five [[economic]] hunches based on Tier 4 sources. Always check [[source-evaluation]] tiers when weighing conflicting findings.

### 3. Name the Tensions
Every synthesis must include a "Contradictions & Tensions" section. If you can't find any, you haven't looked hard enough. See [[contradiction-protocol]].

### 4. Separate Confidence Levels
Not all findings are equally certain. Tag each major claim:
- **High confidence:** Multiple lenses agree, Tier 1-2 sources
- **Medium confidence:** Some lens support, Tier 2-3 sources, or logical inference
- **Low confidence:** Single lens, Tier 3+ sources, or speculative

### 5. Preserve the Surprising Findings
If a lens produces something unexpected, highlight it — don't bury it in a paragraph. Surprising findings are the highest-value output of multi-lens analysis.

### 6. Connect to Prior Knowledge
Check [[concepts]] and [[data-points]] for related findings from past research. Note connections and contradictions with prior work.

### 7. Generate Open Questions
Every synthesis must end with open questions — things this research raised but couldn't answer. These seed future projects and get logged in [[research-log]].

## Anti-Patterns

- **The Smoothie:** Blending everything together into one homogeneous take. Preserve distinct flavors.
- **The Stack:** Just listing findings from each lens without integration. That's a report, not a synthesis.
- **The Cherry Pick:** Leading with your preferred conclusion, then selecting supporting evidence. Let the evidence lead.
- **The Hedge:** "It depends" without specifying on what. Always name the conditions.
